# The Water Cycle: Siklo ng Tubig in an Archipelago

I built the water cycle tool because this is the first big science idea most Filipino kids meet, and it is easy to nod along to without ever really seeing it. So here is the idea in one line. Water keeps moving between the sea, the sky, and the land, around and around, and it has been doing this for billions of years. We call that loop the water cycle, or siklo ng tubig.

The amount of water on Earth barely changes. What changes is where the water is and what form it takes. It can be liquid in the ocean, an invisible gas in the air, or ice on a cold mountain. In a country made of more than seven thousand islands, where the sea is always close and the rain pours for half the year, this loop is about as easy to see as it gets.

The engine that drives the whole thing is the Sun. Let me walk the loop the way the tool does, one step at a time.

First is evaporation, or pagsingaw. The Sun heats the top of the ocean. The heat turns liquid water into a gas called water vapor, and the vapor rises into the air. You cannot see it, but it is there. Plants help too. They pull water up from the soil and let it out through their leaves, which is called transpiration. Over a big forest like the Sierra Madre, that adds a lot of water to the sky.

Next is condensation, or pamumuo ng ulap. As the vapor floats higher, the air gets colder. The cold makes the vapor turn back into tiny drops of water. Billions of these tiny drops floating together are what a cloud actually is.

Then comes precipitation, or pag-ulan. The little drops bump into each other and join up. When a drop gets too heavy for the air to hold, it falls. Most of the time it falls as rain. In very cold places it can fall as snow or hail.

After the rain lands, the tool follows two more steps, runoff and collection. Some water flows downhill across the ground. That is runoff, or pag-agos, and it gathers into creeks and rivers. Some water soaks into the soil and becomes groundwater. In the end the water collects in rivers, lakes, the sea, and underground. A dam like Angat catches some of it to supply Metro Manila. Then the Sun heats it again, and the loop starts over.

## Most of the water is not where you would guess

Here is the part that surprises people. Because water is always moving, it feels like there is plenty to go around. There is not much we can easily use. Almost all of Earth's water, more than 96 percent, is salty ocean water, and we cannot drink that. Only a small slice is fresh water. And most of that fresh water is not in the rivers and lakes we use either.

Look at the chart. About two-thirds of all fresh water is frozen solid in ice caps and glaciers. Almost all the rest is underground. The rivers, lakes, and streams that most homes and farms actually drink from add up to barely more than one percent of the fresh water. That is a number worth sitting with. The water we depend on is a thin layer that the cycle keeps recycling. When the rains stop for a while, that layer drops fast, and a dam like Angat can get worryingly low.

## The cycle you can feel: Habagat and Amihan

What gives the Philippine water cycle its beat is the monsoon. For about half the year, the Habagat (the southwest monsoon) pulls warm, wet air off the sea and dumps heavy rain, sometimes for days. For the other half, the Amihan (the northeast monsoon) brings cooler, drier air down from the north. PAGASA watches the switch between them, because it sets the calendar for planting, for floods, and for the water supply.

This is the part you can check from your own window. The puddle that dries up on a hot tag-init afternoon is evaporation. The fog stuck to a mountain in the morning is condensation. The flood from a Habagat downpour is just precipitation falling faster than the ground and the drains can carry it back to the sea. None of these are separate events. They are the same water, the same loop, caught at different steps.
