# Pythagorean Theorem: Beyond the Triangle

The Pythagorean theorem ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$) is widely taught as a formula to find the missing side of a right triangle. However, the true mathematical rigor lies in understanding that it's actually an equation relating **areas**.

## Dissection of the Variables

### 1. Side $a$ and Side $b$ (The Legs)
- **Unit**: Linear units ($\text{cm}$, $\text{m}$, etc.)
- **What they actually are**: The two sides that intersect to form exactly a $90^\circ$ angle. In coordinate geometry, these often represent the $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ (change in X and change in Y) between two points.

### 2. $a^2$ and $b^2$ (The Areas)
- **Unit**: Square units ($\text{cm}^2$, $\text{m}^2$)
- **What they actually are**: This is the core visual insight. When you square a number geometrically, you are literally constructing a square shape where every side has that length. $a^2$ is the physical 2D area of a square built attached to side $a$. 

### 3. $c$ (The Hypotenuse)
- **What it actually is**: The longest side of the right triangle, always opposite the right angle. In the real world, this is the "straight-line distance" between two points. Because calculating $c$ involves a square root ($c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$), the result is frequently an irrational number (like $\sqrt{98}$).

### 4. Pythagorean Triples
- **What they actually are**: Rare sets of three positive integers $(a, b, c)$ that perfectly satisfy the theorem without producing decimals. The most famous is $3, 4, 5$. If you have a rope with $12$ evenly spaced knots, you can form a $3-4-5$ right triangle. Ancient builders (like the Egyptians) used this exact method to ensure their corners were perfectly square without modern protractors!
