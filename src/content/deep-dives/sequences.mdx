# Sequences: when adding loses to multiplying

I built the sequences tool because the hard part of this topic is not spotting a pattern, it is making the leap from *seeing* one to *writing* it as a rule. That is where a lot of students stall. They can continue $2, 5, 8, 11, \dots$ all day, then freeze when asked for the hundredth term, because continuing means counting and a rule means jumping straight there. A sequence is just an ordered list of numbers that follows a rule, and once you can name the rule you can land on the hundredth term without listing the ninety-nine before it. The two patterns that carry most of the Grade 10 work are the arithmetic and the geometric, and the gap between how they grow is wider than almost anyone expects going in.

Here is the misconception the bar chart is built to break. Students assume a constant multiplier is just "a bit faster" than a constant addition, like two cars at slightly different speeds. It is not a bit faster. Multiplying buries adding so completely that no head start on the adding side can save it. You have to see it to believe it.

## Arithmetic: the same step, every time

In an arithmetic sequence you get the next term by *adding* a fixed amount, the common difference $d$. The sequence $2, 5, 8, 11, 14, \dots$ has $d = 3$, since each term is three more than the one before it. The closed-form rule is

$$a_n = a_1 + (n - 1)d,$$

which reads the way it works. Start at the first term $a_1$, then take $(n - 1)$ steps of size $d$ to reach the $n$-th term. For that sequence the tenth term is $a_{10} = 2 + (10 - 1)\cdot 3 = 2 + 27 = 29$, found in one line instead of nine additions.

The mental model worth keeping is your *alkansya*. Drop the same ₱20 into the jar every day on top of a ₱2 start, and the running total marches up in equal steps. Arithmetic growth is steady, predictable, and linear. Plot the terms and they fall on a straight line whose slope is $d$. Nothing speeds up.

## Geometric: a constant multiplier, and everything changes

A geometric sequence gets its next term by *multiplying* by a fixed number, the common ratio $r$. The sequence $2, 4, 8, 16, 32, \dots$ has $r = 2$, since each term is double the last. Its rule is

$$a_n = a_1 \cdot r^{\,n-1}.$$

Same starting point, but now the tenth term is $a_{10} = 2 \cdot 2^{\,10-1} = 2 \cdot 2^9 = 2 \cdot 512 = 1024$. Hold that next to the arithmetic version, which crawled to only 29 by its tenth term from the identical start of 2.

That is the whole story of exponential growth in one picture. Adding wins early and loses forever. A constant multiplier bigger than 1 eventually swamps any constant addition, no matter how large the step. It is the reason the informal *"5-6"* loan can outrun the income meant to pay it back, since the balance multiplies each cycle while the wages only add. It is also why a forwarded *chain message* or a virus reaches everyone so fast: each round multiplies the last. When $0 < r < 1$ the same machinery runs in reverse, halving toward zero, which is how a bouncing ball's height or a draining battery fades out.

## Reading the rule off the numbers

Most exam problems hand you a few terms and ask for the rule, so the diagnostic is worth drilling until it is automatic. Subtract consecutive terms: if the difference stays constant, it is arithmetic and that constant is $d$. Divide consecutive terms instead: if the ratio stays constant, it is geometric and that constant is $r$. Try a sequence in the tool above, change the starting term and the step or the ratio, and watch the lines either climb in a straight ramp or curl up off the page.

Two cautions that catch people out. First, the exponent in the geometric formula is $n - 1$, not $n$. The first term is multiplied by $r^0 = 1$, which leaves it untouched, exactly as it should. Second, a list of numbers is under no obligation to be arithmetic or geometric at all. The Fibonacci sequence $1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, \dots$, where each term is the sum of the previous two, is neither, and plenty of real patterns are neither. So test before you assume, instead of forcing every list into one of the two tidy boxes.
