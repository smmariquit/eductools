# Photosynthesis: where a tree's mass actually comes from

Ask most people where a tree gets its bulk and they will point at the soil. It is a reasonable guess. We grow by eating solid food, so it follows that a plant must be eating the ground it stands in. The guess is also wrong, and the experiment that disproved it is almost four centuries old. I built the tool that goes with this writeup around two questions a static diagram never really answers: where does the wood actually come from, and why does a plant on a bright, well-watered day still sometimes refuse to grow any faster.

## The Van Helmont willow

In the 1640s the Flemish physician Jan Baptista van Helmont set out to test the soil idea properly. He planted a willow sapling of about 5 pounds in a pot holding 200 pounds of oven-dried soil, covered the pot so no stray dirt could blow in, and for five years gave it nothing but water. At the end he pulled the tree out and weighed it: roughly 169 pounds. Then he dried the soil again. It had lost barely two ounces.

Van Helmont concluded that the new wood had come from the water, which was half right and was about as far as anyone could get without knowing that air is made of anything. The missing 164 pounds did not come from the soil and did not all come from the water either. Most of it was pulled out of the air. The Van Helmont Mass Balance view in the tool reruns his five years on fast-forward: the plant's biomass climbs while the dry-soil figure barely twitches, which is the whole point made visible.

Through carbon fixation, a leaf takes in atmospheric carbon dioxide, strips out the carbon, and uses it to build glucose and then cellulose, the structural sugar that makes up most of the dry weight of wood. The narra or acacia shading a schoolyard is, in a literal sense, assembled mostly from a gas. The overall reaction is the one every Grade 7 learner eventually copies into a notebook:

$$ 6\,CO_2 + 6\,H_2O \;\xrightarrow{\text{light}}\; C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6\,O_2 $$

Six molecules of carbon dioxide and six of water, driven by light, become one of glucose and six of oxygen. The oxygen is, from the plant's point of view, a waste product. It is also most of the reason we are here.

## The gates in the leaf: stomatal gas exchange

A leaf cannot fix carbon it cannot reach, and the carbon is outside. To get at it, plants rely on microscopic pores called *stomata*, concentrated on the shaded underside of most leaves. Each pore sits between two guard cells that open and close it.

The mechanism is hydraulic rather than muscular. When water and light are plentiful, the guard cells actively pump in potassium ions; water follows by osmosis, the cells swell and bow apart, and the pore gapes open. Carbon dioxide diffuses in while oxygen and water vapour diffuse out. The same opening that lets carbon in lets water escape, and that trade-off is the whole problem. In a drought the plant has to choose between keeping the stomata open to keep feeding, at the risk of drying out, or sealing them shut to conserve water and starving its own photosynthesis in the process. Plants in hot, dry conditions tend to err toward closing, which is one reason growth slows long before a plant visibly wilts.

## Limiting factors: why the rate hits a ceiling

Photosynthesis is not a dial you can turn up without end. It runs at the speed set by whatever resource is scarcest at that moment, an idea usually credited to F. F. Blackman and known as the law of limiting factors. The tool's other view, Stomatal Gas Exchange, hands you three sliders (light, carbon dioxide, and soil water) and a running net rate, so you can hunt for the bottleneck yourself: push light to the ceiling and the rate still stalls if the carbon dioxide slider is low. Two factors dominate that model.

Light is the first. In dim conditions, adding light raises the rate more or less in proportion: more photons, more reactions. But the photosynthetic machinery has a fixed throughput, and once it saturates, extra light does nothing. The rate flattens into a plateau. The visualizer models that curve with a non-rectangular hyperbola, which bends from the initial straight-line response into the ceiling set by the leaf's quantum yield.

Carbon dioxide is the second, and it can be limiting even under blinding light. The enzyme that grabs CO₂, Rubisco, can only work as fast as carbon arrives. We model that dependence with Michaelis–Menten kinetics, the standard description of an enzyme whose rate climbs with substrate concentration and then levels off as the enzyme saturates:

$$ v = \frac{V_{max}\,[CO_2]}{K_m + [CO_2]} $$

Here $V_{max}$ is the ceiling rate and $K_m$ is the CO₂ concentration at which the reaction runs at half that ceiling. The shape is the same plateau again: double the carbon when carbon is scarce and the rate climbs; double it when Rubisco is already saturated and almost nothing happens. Whichever factor is in shortest supply is the one setting the speed, which is exactly the point Blackman was making.

In the Stomatal Gas Exchange view you can watch that trade-off directly. The two green guard cells swing apart as water and light rise, and particles stream through the gap: grey dots are the carbon dioxide (CO₂) coming in, red dots the oxygen (O₂) heading out. Drag the water slider down and the pore closes, the particles stop, and the net rate drops to zero no matter how bright you make the light.
