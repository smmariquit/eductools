# Reading a Typhoon the Way PAGASA Does

I built the typhoon tracker because the labels on a storm bulletin are the closest thing we have to a national second language, and most people learn them the hard way, mid-storm, when it is too late to look anything up. An average of about twenty tropical cyclones enter the Philippine Area of Responsibility each year, the box the tool draws on the map and labels PAR, and somewhere between eight and nine of them make landfall. That makes the Philippines one of the most cyclone-exposed countries on Earth, and it makes the gap between a "tropical depression" and a "super typhoon" worth understanding before you need it. The names are not bureaucratic trivia. Each one is a specific wind-speed threshold that tells you roughly how much damage to brace for.

## How a low-pressure area becomes a typhoon

A tropical cyclone is, underneath everything, a heat engine running on warm seawater. It starts as a low-pressure area, a patch of ocean where air is rising and the surface pressure is lower than its surroundings. Over water warm enough, near or above 26 to 27 degrees, that rising air hauls up enormous amounts of water vapor, and when the vapor condenses high in the atmosphere it releases latent heat. That heat warms the column, drops the pressure further, and pulls in still more moist air, which condenses and releases still more heat. Left alone over warm water with little wind shear to tear it apart, the loop feeds itself and the winds spiral faster and faster around the deepening low. Slide the wind speed up in the tool and you can watch the pressure reading fall as the category climbs, which is the same relationship run backward.

This is also why typhoons fall apart quickly once they cross land or cooler water: cut the warm-water fuel and the engine stalls. The same physics explains why our typhoon season tracks the warm-ocean months, and why a storm can strengthen alarmingly fast over the open Philippine Sea before it ever reaches the coast.

## The five categories, by wind speed

PAGASA sorts tropical cyclones by their maximum sustained winds, and it revised the scale in March 2022, adding a severe-tropical-storm tier and lowering the super-typhoon threshold from the old 220 km/h down to 185 km/h to line up with international practice. A low-pressure area that tightens into a closed circulation with winds up to 62 km/h is a tropical depression; above that, the named categories begin.

So a tropical storm runs 62 to 88 km/h, a severe tropical storm 89 to 117, a typhoon 118 to 184, and anything at 185 km/h or above is a super typhoon. The thresholds are deliberately not evenly spaced, because wind damage is not linear. The force a wind exerts climbs with the square of its speed, so the step from a tropical storm to a typhoon is a far bigger leap in destructive power than the bare numbers suggest. It is also why the tool's storm-surge estimate and the size of the swirl on the map both jump as you push the slider up.

One technical detail trips people up when they compare forecasts from different agencies. PAGASA measures maximum sustained wind as a 10-minute average, while the US scale used for Atlantic hurricanes, the Saffir-Simpson scale, uses a 1-minute average, which reads higher for the very same storm. That is why one system can look like it is in two different categories on two agencies' maps. They are clocking the same wind over different stopwatches.

## Categories are not the same as signals

Here is the distinction I built the whole tool around, because it is the one that actually keeps people safe. The storm's category is one thing. The Tropical Cyclone Wind Signals, the No. 1 through No. 5 warnings PAGASA raises over specific places, are another. The category describes the storm itself, out over the water. The signal describes the wind expected at a given town as the storm approaches, and that is the number that should drive whether you secure the roof, move the boats, or evacuate. A weak storm passing directly overhead can earn a higher local signal than a monster sitting far offshore. The tool shows both on purpose, the category in the storm's readout and the signal number stepping from 1 to 5 as the wind climbs, so the habit students build is the right one: track the storm to understand it, but act on the signal raised over your own town.

And then act early, which is the whole reason the tool ends in a before, during, and after checklist rather than a score. The useful work happens before landfall: packing the go-bag, knowing your evacuation center and whether your area floods, charging the power banks. It continues after the winds drop, when the quieter hazards arrive, like floodwater hiding deep currents, downed power lines, and the leptospirosis that comes from wading through standing water. The storm's category is what makes the news. The signal and the checklist are what get a household through it.

## A couple of questions people ask

**Why does a weaker storm sometimes get a higher signal in my town than a stronger one?**
Because the signal is about your location, not the storm's headline category. A modest storm crossing directly over you can put more wind on your street than a violent one staying far out at sea. Always act on the signal raised over your own area, not the category in the national headline.

**Did the super-typhoon number really change?**
Yes. In March 2022 PAGASA lowered the super-typhoon threshold from 220 km/h to 185 km/h and added the severe-tropical-storm tier, so older charts and newer ones do not always match. The tool uses the current 2022 bands.
