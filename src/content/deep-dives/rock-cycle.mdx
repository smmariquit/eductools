# The Rock Cycle, Read Off Mayon and Taal


Most textbooks draw the rock cycle as a tidy circle of arrows, and I think that picture quietly teaches the wrong thing. The arrows look like a one-way conveyor belt, a fixed sequence a rock has to follow in order. So I built the rock cycle tool to make the shortcuts as obvious as the main loop, and the reason it works here is that the Philippines is one of the few places you can point out the window and see all three rock families being made, broken, and remade on a human timescale, sometimes uncomfortably fast.



Start with the three families, the way the tool steps through them. Igneous rock forms when molten material cools and crystallizes, whether that is lava chilling on a Mayon slope or magma freezing slowly a few kilometers down. Sedimentary rock forms when older rock is weathered into grains, carried off by rivers and waves, then buried and cemented back together; the limestone of the Puerto Princesa Underground River in Palawan is exactly this, built from ancient marine sediment. Metamorphic rock is the in-between case, existing rock cooked and squeezed by heat and pressure until its minerals recrystallize without ever fully melting; Romblon marble is just limestone that went through that. The arrows between them are the part that actually matters, and here is the correction: any rock can become any other rock given enough heat, pressure, weathering, or time. The tool spells this out, that igneous rock can be metamorphosed directly, and that any rock can be weathered back to sediment or melted back to magma. That is why "cycle" is the right word and "sequence" is not.

## The same loop, running faster here

What makes the Philippine version worth teaching is the speed of the igneous step. Sitting on an active subduction zone means fresh igneous rock is delivered to the surface on a regular basis, and the weathering machine that turns it back into sediment runs hard, because we get a great deal of rain. Volcanic ash and broken-down lava weather into the deep, fertile soils that farmers in Albay and Batangas plant on, which is the sedimentary half of the cycle doing quiet, useful work between eruptions.

The metamorphic step is the hard one to see, because it happens out of sight. As one tectonic plate slides beneath another, rock gets dragged down into higher temperature and crushing pressure, and its minerals reorganize into denser, often layered forms. Some of that material eventually melts and feeds back up as fresh magma, closing the loop. So the same plate boundary that builds our volcanoes is also quietly running the metamorphic and re-melting stages a little deeper down.

## Why Mayon and Taal don't erupt the same way

Here is where chemistry earns its seat in an earth-science lesson. Volcanic rocks are sorted largely by how much silica ($\mathrm{SiO_2}$) they contain, and silica content tracks closely with how stiff the magma is, which in turn sets how a volcano behaves. Low-silica magma is runny, lets gas escape easily, and tends to erupt as flowing lava. High-silica magma is thick and sticky, traps its gas, and is far more prone to blowing out explosively.

Mayon's products sit in the basaltic-andesite-to-andesite range, the middle of that ladder, which is part of why it produces both lava flows and the fast, deadly avalanches of hot ash and rock called pyroclastic flows. Now the misconception I most want to head off: people assume low-silica always means a gentle eruption. Taal trends toward the lower-silica, basaltic end, and it has still managed savagely explosive eruptions. The reason is that at Taal the magma meets the water of the lake and crater, and the flash to steam adds a punch that silica content alone would never predict. The lesson worth keeping is that composition sets the tendency, not the verdict. Real volcanoes also depend on how much gas is dissolved in the magma and on whether that magma runs into groundwater on the way up.

For students, the payoff is that the rock cycle stops being a diagram on a page. The andesite in a Mayon boulder, the ash-rich soil of a Bicol rice paddy, the Palawan limestone, and the Romblon marble are all stages of one process, and the volcano they can see from the highway is simply the part of the loop that happens to run on a timescale a person can actually watch.
