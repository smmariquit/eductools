# Electric Circuits: Ohm's Law, Series, and Parallel

Every December I lose an evening to the same problem: a string of parol lights that worked last year refuses to light this year, and the culprit is always one dead bulb taking the whole string down with it. That single annoyance is the reason I built this circuit tool, because it is the cleanest example of an idea that confuses a lot of students. How you wire things together changes everything, even when the parts are identical. The visualizer above gives you two identical bulbs, a battery, and a switch, and lets you flip between two ways of wiring them so you can watch the same parts behave completely differently.

Start with what a circuit even is. A circuit is just a loop that gives electric charge somewhere to go. Connect a battery to a bulb with an unbroken path of metal wire, and tiny charges drift around the loop, dropping their energy in the bulb as light and heat. Break the loop anywhere, like flicking the switch in the tool to OFF, and everything stops at once. This is Grade 8 territory in the Philippine science curriculum, and most of it comes down to one equation and two ways of connecting things.

## Three quantities, one equation

Three quantities describe what is happening in a simple circuit, and Ohm's law links all three:

$$ V = I \, R $$

Voltage $V$, measured in volts, is the push the battery provides, the energy it gives to each bit of charge. Current $I$, measured in amperes (amps for short), is how fast charge actually flows past a point, where one ampere means one unit of charge per second. Resistance $R$, measured in ohms (the symbol is $\Omega$), is how hard the path fights that flow. Rearrange the equation as $I = V/R$ and it reads the way your intuition wants it to: raise the voltage and more current flows, raise the resistance and less does. A long thin wire resists more than a short fat one, the same way a crowd squeezes through a narrow door more slowly than a wide one.

One honest caution. Ohm's law describes the parts we call resistors well, where doubling the voltage really does double the current. It is not a deep law of nature the way Newton's laws are. A real light bulb's filament changes its resistance as it heats up, so it only loosely obeys it, which is why the tool keeps the bulb resistance fixed to keep the lesson clean.

## Series and parallel are two different bargains

How you connect the components decides how the voltage and current get divided up, and this is the part worth getting backwards before you get it right. Most people guess that adding a second bulb to a circuit always makes things dimmer. Sometimes it does, sometimes it does the opposite, and which one happens depends entirely on the wiring.

In a series circuit, everything sits on a single path, one after another, so the same current flows through every component and the total resistance is just the sum, $R_{\text{series}} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots$. The catch is that one path means one point of failure. If a bulb burns out, the loop is broken and the whole line goes dark. That cheap parol string that dies every December is wired exactly this way. In the tool, switch to series and notice that each bulb only gets half the battery's voltage, so the brightness readout drops: two bulbs sharing one push means neither gets the full shove.

A parallel circuit gives each component its own branch across the same two points, so each one sees the full battery voltage and the current splits among the branches. Adding more branches gives the current more routes to take, so the total resistance actually drops below the smallest single resistor:

$$ \frac{1}{R_{\text{parallel}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots $$

Because the branches are independent, one bulb failing leaves the others lit. Flip the tool to parallel and watch both bulbs jump to full brightness while the total current the battery supplies climbs. This is why house wiring is parallel: every outlet in your home gets the same $230\text{ V}$, and unplugging the rice cooker does not switch off the lights.

## Reading a real circuit

Put the two ideas together and you can reason about almost any simple board. Find the series and parallel groups, collapse each one into a single equivalent resistance, then use $I = V/R$ to get the total current the battery supplies. From there you work backward: components in series share the current, branches in parallel share the voltage. That bookkeeping, more than any single formula, is what the circuit visualizer is built to make visible, which is why it shows you the per-bulb voltage and per-bulb power and not just one tidy number.

## A few questions I actually get

**Why does one dead bulb kill my whole parol string?**
Because the string is wired in series, on a single loop. When one bulb burns out it breaks the loop, and a broken loop carries no current, so every other bulb goes dark too. House wiring avoids this by using parallel branches instead.

**If I add a second bulb, does the circuit get dimmer or brighter?**
It depends on the wiring. Add it in series and the two bulbs share the voltage, so each one dims. Add it in parallel and each bulb still gets the full battery voltage, so both stay bright. The tool lets you flip between the two and watch the brightness readout change.

**Why is my house wired in parallel instead of series?**
So that each appliance gets the full mains voltage and works on its own. In parallel, unplugging or switching off one device leaves the others running, and a single failure does not take down the whole house. Series wiring would mean one blown bulb could darken every room.
