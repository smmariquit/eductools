# Electromagnetic Spectrum: The Part You Actually Live Inside

When I built the spectrum slider, I assumed the interesting end was the dangerous one. Gamma rays, X-rays, the bands with skulls on the warning labels. Then I watched a few students drag the slider around, and the part that made them stop was nowhere near that end. It was the quiet middle, the radio and microwave bands, the moment one of them realized that the same physics carrying a DZMM broadcast also carries the GCash transfer she had just sent. That strip is where most Filipinos actually live, and almost nobody gets taught to see it that way.

Stringing fiber across more than 7,000 islands is slow, expensive work, so for most people here the internet does not arrive through a cable in the ground. It arrives as invisible waves through the air, handed off from a cell tower on a hill to the phone in your pocket. The Messenger call to a kapatid working abroad, the typhoon advisory, the load you bought at the sari-sari store this morning, all of it rides on a thin and crowded strip of electromagnetic radiation that engineers argue over by the megahertz. So this writeup is less about the dramatic ends of the slider and more about the band you are sitting inside right now.

## Higher frequency is not simply "better"

Here is the part most people get backwards. When a phone shows 5G and the speed test jumps, the easy conclusion is that higher frequency is just better, and that the telco is being stingy whenever you cannot get it. The physics is less convenient than that. Radio and microwave frequencies are a finite resource, parceled out by regulators, and the bands are not interchangeable. Lower frequencies have longer wavelengths that bend around hills and push through walls and rain, so one tower covers a lot of ground while carrying comparatively little data. Higher frequencies carry far more data, which is exactly what makes them attractive for fast mobile internet, but their short waves are blocked by almost anything and fade quickly over distance.

That trade-off is the whole story of mobile coverage in the country. A telco rolling out service to a remote town in Mindanao leans on lower bands, around 700 MHz, because a single tower can blanket a wide, thinly populated area. In Makati or Cebu City, where demand is dense, they pack in many towers running higher bands so each one serves a small cell with plenty of capacity. When people complain that 5G is blazing but only works near the mall, they are bumping into wavelength, not a billing conspiracy. Drag the slider on the tool toward higher frequency and watch the wavelength readout collapse: that shrinking number is the coverage you are trading away for speed.

## The microwave band does the heavy lifting

Most of the wireless technology you touch every day actually lives in the microwave portion of the spectrum, the highest-frequency radio waves that ordinary circuits can still generate cheaply. Cell signals, WiFi, Bluetooth, and satellite links all crowd into this region. Satellites favor it for a good reason: some microwaves pass cleanly through clouds, which makes them dependable for beaming signals up and down through Philippine weather that is rarely in the mood to cooperate. The slider even labels this band as the one that warms your leftover adobo, which is not a joke so much as a hint.

Because here is the part that surprises people. The microwave oven reheating that adobo and the router serving your WiFi are tuned to almost the same frequency.

A microwave oven runs at 2.45 GHz because that frequency makes water molecules twist back and forth and heat up, which is what cooks the food. Older WiFi uses 2.4 GHz, right next door. The two sit close enough that a cheap or leaky oven really can drop your connection while it runs, which is one practical reason newer routers also offer a 5 GHz band: more room, less interference, shorter range. None of this is exotic. It is the same spectrum, sliced for different jobs, and the jobs sometimes step on each other.

## The old broadcast bands still hold the line

For all the attention paid to mobile data, the oldest broadcast bands have not retired, and in a disaster they earn their keep. AM radio occupies 540 to 1600 kHz and FM runs from 88 to 108 MHz, low enough that a single transmitter reaches across provinces and a battery-powered receiver keeps working when the cell network and the power grid have both gone down in a storm. During the worst typhoons, that humble FM signal is sometimes the only thing still talking to a cut-off barangay. The dramatic end of the slider gets the warning labels, but it is this unglamorous low end that you want when the lines are down.

All of it, the oven and the router and the cell tower and the FM station and the satellite passing overhead, is the same electromagnetic radiation moving at the speed of light, separated only by frequency. We did not invent these waves. We learned to label the bands and take turns.

## A few questions I actually get

**Why is my 5G so much faster than my 4G but cuts out the second I walk inside?**
Because the high-frequency bands that make 5G fast have short wavelengths that walls, rain, and distance soak up easily. You are not imagining the drop, and the telco is not throttling you out of spite. It is the same trade-off the slider shows: more data per second, less reach.

**Can my microwave oven really mess up my WiFi?**
Yes, if your router is on the 2.4 GHz band, because the oven runs at 2.45 GHz, almost the same frequency. A well-shielded oven leaks very little, but a cheap or aging one can nudge your connection while it runs. Moving the router to the 5 GHz band usually settles it.

**If radio is such an old technology, why do we still bother with it?**
Because reach beats speed when the grid is down. A low-frequency FM signal covers a province from one transmitter and a battery radio keeps receiving it through a typhoon. That is not nostalgia, it is the physics of long wavelengths doing exactly what the fast bands cannot.
