# What's actually plugged in at the other end


I built our first classroom simulation thinking the hard part was the physics. Then I watched a teacher in a real public school try to run it, and the projector that was supposed to display it flickered out halfway through the lesson. The science was fine. The Tuesday it ran on was the whole story. Ever since, I have spent a strange amount of time on a question most edtech skips right past: what is actually plugged in at the other end? You can write the most careful tool in the world, and it still runs on whatever the school happens to have that day.



The ambition isn't the problem. It's written into the law. The [Enhanced Basic Education Act of 2013](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2013/ra_10533_2013.html) put information and communications technology and "21st-century skills" into the curriculum as policy, not aspiration, and it told schools to contextualize and localize their teaching to the learners in front of them. That is the right instinct. It also quietly assumes a floor of electricity, devices, connectivity, and teacher time that a great many schools simply do not stand on. So before I talk about tools, I want to be honest about the floor, and trace every number to the people who actually counted it, so you can check me.

## The numbers nobody enjoys quoting

When the Philippines sat the OECD's PISA test for the first time in 2018, the results were hard to read and harder to spin. The [OECD's own country note](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/programmes/edu/pisa/publications/national-reports/pisa-2018/featured-country-specific-overviews/PISA2018_CN_PHL.pdf) recorded an average reading score of 340 and found that more than eight in ten Filipino fifteen-year-olds did not reach even a baseline level of reading proficiency. The World Bank, working from the same data, put it more bluntly: the country [placed last in reading among the 79 participating systems](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/184251593328815913/pdf/Main-Report.pdf), and second to last in mathematics and science.

You can argue about how much weight one international test deserves, and people reasonably do. What's harder to wave away is that the domestic picture says the same thing. The World Bank's [learning poverty measure](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/972851624613012971/pdf/Philippines-Learning-Poverty-Brief-2021.pdf) is just the share of ten-year-olds who can't read and understand a simple, age-appropriate story, and it sat at roughly 90 percent during the pandemic years, up from an already-high 69.5 percent in 2019. The gap doesn't close on its own as kids get older. When DepEd ran its first Senior High School Literacy and Numeracy Assessment in March 2026, the Second Congressional Commission on Education reported that [87 percent of the Grade 11 students assessed were not independent readers](https://edcom2.gov.ph/improvements-seen-but-half-of-learners-still-below-grade-level-in-literacy/), with only about 13 percent able to read a text at their own grade level and actually understand it. These are seventeen-year-olds two years from a diploma.

A tool that animates photosynthesis does not fix any of that. I say it plainly because the temptation in this sector is to pretend otherwise, and I have caught myself doing it.

## The divide was never only about hardware

Here is the part almost everyone gets backwards. We call all of this "the digital divide," which makes it sound like a hardware problem you could buy your way out of. Procure enough laptops, string enough fiber, and the divide closes. It doesn't. Anyone who has watched a software-dependent lesson collapse because the one classroom projector gave out already knows why.

There are at least two more gaps hiding behind the hardware one. The first is software and licensing. A student told to submit a digital output, edit an image, or run a statistics package is quietly being told to buy a proprietary license their family may not have. The realistic alternatives are an unaffordable subscription or a pirated installer that drags malware onto a shared school machine. Capable free tools exist for nearly all of this, LibreOffice and GIMP and GeoGebra among them, but knowing they exist, trusting them, and matching the right one to a low-bandwidth, low-spec device is its own kind of literacy, and most schools have no one on staff to provide it.

The second gap is the one people are least comfortable naming, because it sounds like blaming teachers, which it is not. Most Filipino teachers can operate the devices fine. What they have rarely been given is time and subject-specific training to fold a tool into a genuine inquiry-based lesson instead of using it as an expensive chalkboard that displays a fixed image faster. That isn't a character flaw. It's what happens when you hand someone a new mandate, a packed list of competencies, an overcrowded room, and a stack of administrative reports, then ask them to redesign their pedagogy on their own time.

## MATATAG landed on top of all this

The government's response to the learning crisis is the MATATAG curriculum, introduced in 2023, which thins out an overloaded competency list and tries to refocus the early grades on foundational reading and numeracy. Whether it works is an empirical question, and the early evidence is genuinely mixed. That is the honest way to describe it, not a reason to cheer or to dismiss.

DepEd's research partner, the Philippine Institute for Development Studies, ran a [cluster-randomized evaluation of the first pilot year across 70 schools](https://serp-p.pids.gov.ph/publication/public/view?slug=promise-and-growing-pains-in-curriculum-reform-a-mixed-methods-evaluation-of-matatag-k-to-10-curri). Grade 2 students in the pilot posted large, statistically significant gains across subjects, which the authors themselves flag as unusual for a first year, since reforms normally take several years to show up in scores. That is a real and encouraging result. The same study found that teachers' physiological wellbeing declined over the year, most likely because the materials arrived late and they ended up building lessons from scratch on their own hours. One pilot year is not proof that the curriculum has turned the corner, and the people who ran the study are the first to say so.

The financial backdrop didn't help the rollout. The 2018 Mandanas ruling and the [full-devolution order that followed it](https://lawphil.net/executive/execord/eo2021/eo_138_2021.html) moved a larger share of national taxes, plus a set of service responsibilities, down to local governments. That shift is defensible on its own terms, but it lands unevenly. A well-resourced city can absorb new duties in a way a last-mile island school's local government cannot, which means a reform that needs steady, even support is being implemented across very uneven ground.

## Where a free browser tool honestly fits

None of this is an argument that digital tools don't matter. It's an argument for being precise about what they can carry. A free, offline-capable simulation that runs on a cheap phone is genuinely useful for the school that has never had a physics lab and never will, and for the teacher who wants students moving a variable around instead of memorizing a diagram. It is close to useless for a Grade 11 student who cannot yet read the instructions, which is exactly why the reading crisis has to be named first.

The state's current bet on recovery is the [Academic Recovery and Accessible Learning Program](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2024/ra_12028_2024.html), signed in 2024, which sets up free tutorials in reading, math, and science for struggling learners from Kindergarten through Grade 10. EDCOM 2's own reviews of its early rollout have been candid about the rough edges, from a heavy reliance on volunteer teachers to errors in the learning materials, which is roughly what a first year of any national program looks like.

So we build to fit the floor, not the ceiling. Small files, works offline, runs on the phone the household already owns, asks nothing of a license. That won't close a divide this structural, and I won't pretend it will. It's the most useful thing a tool can actually do while the harder, slower work of teachers, materials, and connectivity gets done by people with budgets a lot bigger than ours.

## A few questions I actually get

**If the tools can't fix the reading crisis, why build them at all?**
Because "can't fix everything" is not the same as "useless." A simulation that runs offline on a borrowed phone gives a lab-less school something it had nothing of before. It just has to sit behind the reading work, not in front of it.

**Is the digital divide just about internet access?**
No, and that's the trap. Connectivity is the visible layer. Underneath it sit software and licensing costs, and the time and training a teacher needs to use any of it well. You can close the connectivity gap and still leave the other two wide open.

**Did MATATAG already fail, or already work?**
Neither, yet. The first pilot year showed real Grade 2 gains and real strain on teachers, both in the same [PIDS evaluation](https://serp-p.pids.gov.ph/publication/public/view?slug=promise-and-growing-pains-in-curriculum-reform-a-mixed-methods-evaluation-of-matatag-k-to-10-curri). One year is too early to call. Anyone selling you certainty in either direction is selling something.
